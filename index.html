Enceladus:

Enceladus is one of Saturn's natural satellites and is known for its bright, icy surface. Scientists study Enceladus because it has a large ocean of liquid water beneath its icy crust. It also sprays jets of water vapour, ice, and other materials into space from cracks near its south pole. These discoveries make Enceladus one of the best places to search for signs of possible life beyond Earth.

Scientists believe Enceladus formed about 4.5 billion years ago from the cloud of gas, dust, and ice that surrounded Saturn while the planet was forming. Gravity pulled this material together to create the moon. Enceladus is made mostly of water ice with a rocky core. Heat from inside the moon keeps part of the underground ocean in a liquid state, even though the surface is extremely cold.

Enceladus has a smooth, bright surface covered in clean ice, making it one of the most reflective objects in the Solar System. It also has craters, icy plains, and long cracks called "tiger stripes" near its south pole, where the water vapour and ice jets erupt. Enceladus has a diameter of about 504 kilometres and orbits Saturn at a distance of about 238,000 kilometres. It takes about 1.4 Earth days to complete one orbit around Saturn.
